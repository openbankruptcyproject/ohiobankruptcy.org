The Means Test for Columbus Filers

The means test determines Chapter 7 eligibility by comparing your income to the Ohio median.

How It Works

  1. Step 1: Compare 6-month average income to Ohio median. Below = you pass.
  2. Step 2: If above, deduct allowed expenses. Low enough disposable income = you still qualify.

Income Excluded

  • Social Security benefits
  • Veterans' disability payments

If You Do Not Pass

  • File Chapter 13 (no means test)
  • Wait for your 6-month average to drop
  • Argue special circumstances

How the Means Test Actually Works for Columbus Filers

Before you can file Chapter 7 in the Southern District of Ohio, you have to pass the means test. The test is designed to stop high-income debtors from wiping out debts they could afford to pay, but in practice most Columbus filers pass without difficulty because the Ohio median income is below the national average. The two-step test compares your household income to the Ohio median, and if that fails, to your disposable income after allowed expenses.

Step one is the median income comparison. The court adds up every dollar of gross income your household received in the six calendar months before the month you file, doubles it to annualize, and compares it to the Ohio median for your household size. Social Security benefits -- retirement, disability, and SSI -- are excluded from this calculation. So are VA disability benefits. Everything else counts, including unemployment, rental income, spouse income, overtime, bonuses, and contributions from non-household members who help pay your bills.

Step two is only reached if step one fails. Here you fill out Form 122A-2 and subtract IRS allowances for food, clothing, housekeeping, personal care, transportation operating costs, and housing. The IRS Local Standards for Franklin County, Ohio set specific numbers for rent/mortgage and utilities -- check the U.S. Trustee website for the current figures. You also deduct actual amounts for health insurance, taxes, childcare, court-ordered support, secured debt payments coming due in the next 60 months, and priority tax debt. What remains is your monthly disposable income. If it is under a threshold set by statute, you still pass Chapter 7.

Why Columbus Filers Usually Pass

The Southern District of Ohio bankruptcy court sees thousands of Chapter 7 cases each year, and the overwhelming majority pass the means test at step one. The Ohio median has historically sat below both the national average and the incomes of most filers who actually need bankruptcy relief. If you are considering Chapter 7 after a job loss, medical crisis, or divorce, run your last six months of bank deposits through the calculator before assuming you do not qualify. And if you just got a new job that pushes you above median, the income that matters is what you actually earned in the lookback window, not what you are projected to earn going forward.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if I file at the end of the month instead of the beginning?

Timing the filing date can matter a lot. The six-month lookback always ends on the last day of the calendar month before your filing month. If you file on February 28, your lookback is August through January. If you file on March 1, your lookback is September through February. That shift can drop a high-income month out of the window and a low-income month in, changing whether you pass median.

Does Social Security count for the Columbus means test?

No. Social Security retirement, SSDI, and SSI benefits are all excluded from current monthly income on Form 122A-1. The same is true for payments to victims of war crimes and crimes against humanity. VA disability benefits are also excluded.

What counts as 'household size' in the Columbus means test?

The Sixth Circuit generally uses a 'heads on beds' approach -- everyone who lives in your home counts, including adult children, roommates who share expenses, elderly parents, and unrelated dependents. Some trustees push back on non-dependents, but the prevailing rule is physical residence plus shared household economics, not IRS dependency status.
