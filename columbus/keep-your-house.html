Keeping Your House in Bankruptcy - Columbus, Ohio

Ohio's $145,425 homestead exemption and Chapter 13 foreclosure defense protect most Columbus homeowners.

Ohio's Homestead Exemption

$145,425 of equity protected. Doubles for joint filers ($290,850). Equity = home value minus mortgage balance.

Chapter 7

Home protected if equity is under the exemption. Must stay current on mortgage - Chapter 7 does not catch up arrears.

Chapter 13: Stop Foreclosure

  • Automatic stay stops foreclosure immediately
  • Catch up missed payments over 3-5 years
  • Keep home regardless of equity
  • Second mortgages may be stripped

Keeping Your House in a Columbus Bankruptcy

For most Columbus homeowners, keeping the house is the top priority in any bankruptcy filing. The good news: bankruptcy was not designed to take your home. Between the homestead exemption and the Chapter 13 cure provisions, the great majority of filers keep their houses and come out the other side with their mortgage current and their unsecured debts discharged. Here is how it works.

The first question is whether you are current on your mortgage. If you are, and your equity is within the Ohio homestead exemption, you can file Chapter 7 and simply keep making your mortgage payments throughout the case. The mortgage lender cannot accelerate the loan just because you filed - ipso facto clauses are unenforceable under 11 U.S.C. section 365(e). This is called "riding through": you keep paying, the lender keeps accepting, and the lien survives the discharge.

If you are behind on the mortgage and the lender has started or is about to start foreclosure, Chapter 13 is almost always the better choice. Chapter 13 lets you "cure" a mortgage arrearage over the full length of your plan - up to 60 months - while keeping your regular monthly payment current. So if you owe $18,000 in back payments and you have a 60-month plan, you add $300 per month to your Chapter 13 payment to catch up the arrearage, and the bank cannot foreclose as long as you stay current on both the arrearage and the ongoing mortgage. This is one of Chapter 13's superpowers.

Equity and the Homestead Exemption

If your home equity exceeds your Ohio homestead exemption, a Chapter 7 trustee could theoretically sell the house, pay you the exempt portion, and distribute the rest to unsecured creditors. In practice, trustees rarely actually sell houses - the transaction costs (commissions, carrying costs, title issues) often eat up any recovery. But if your non-exempt equity is substantial, the trustee may demand a buy-back payment or push you into Chapter 13. In Chapter 13, non-exempt equity gets paid to unsecureds through the plan over three to five years, and you keep the house.

Second Mortgages and Lien Stripping

If you have a second mortgage or HELOC and your first mortgage balance is higher than the home's current market value, you may be able to "strip" the second lien in Chapter 13. A wholly unsecured junior lien can be voided and treated as unsecured debt, which usually means paying pennies on the dollar through the plan. This does not work in Chapter 7 - Dewsnup v. Timm (1992) forbids lien stripping in Chapter 7 - and it does not work if the second is even one dollar in the money.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will filing bankruptcy in Columbus trigger foreclosure?

No. The opposite. Filing triggers the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. section 362, which immediately halts any pending foreclosure sale, stops lender calls, and freezes all collection activity. If a foreclosure sale is scheduled for tomorrow, filing today stops it. The lender has to get relief from the stay before proceeding, which takes weeks or months and gives you time to cure the arrearage through Chapter 13.

Can I discharge my mortgage in a Columbus Chapter 7?

You can discharge your personal liability on the mortgage note, but the lien on the house survives. That means if you stop paying after discharge, the lender can foreclose on the collateral but cannot sue you for a deficiency. This is sometimes useful for underwater houses you plan to surrender - no risk of a deficiency lawsuit post-discharge.

How much home equity can I protect in Ohio?

The Ohio homestead exemption amount varies by state and is updated periodically. Some states allow only a few thousand dollars, others allow hundreds of thousands, and a handful (Florida, Texas, Kansas, Iowa, Oklahoma, South Dakota) allow unlimited equity subject to acreage caps. Always check the current Ohio statute before assuming you are covered.
